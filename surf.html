THE TOP SURF SPOTS

Jaws

Jaws is three miles east of Pāʻia and is considered the most significant and baddest surf spot in all of Hawaii. With waves that are rumored to reach 120 feet, the reef break was largely inaccessible to surfers before the advent of tow-in surfing, led by Laird Hamilton. However the impressive area’s popularity has actually brought an inflow of unskilled internet users, angering residents who need to share priceless realty and fret about injuries.

Ho‘okipa

There are four distinct surf breaks at Ho'okipa. Pavilions is the break furthest east, off the lookout parking. West from it, facing the main parking, is Middles break. Usually these are both left to surfers. The area between the two, which catches fewer breaking sets, is referred to as Girlie Bowl or Green Trees. Next further west, facing the lifeguard tower, is H‘Poko or Point. It is the most popular break for windsurfing, and generally breaks as a right.

Ma’alaea Pipeline

Situated 15 miles south of Lahaina, near Kihai, the Ma’alaea Pipeline in Ma’alaea Bay on the west coast is known for its superfine, very fast surf. Here the seab is made of sharp coral and lava rocks, angled at just the right degree to produce fine waves. It is considered to be one of the world's fastest rideable waves, but is rideable only on large southerly swells. In winter, migrating humpback whales visit the harbor of the small fishing village.

Honolua Bay

Honolua Bay on Maui's northwest shore is a favorite spot for experienced surfers. During the winter high surf season, Honolua has been known to have a hollow, powerful wave that offers incredibly long rides. The bluffs above the bay offer a great vantage point for visitors to watch the pros from a safe distance. Surfing lessons are available, but note that during the winter, wave conditions at Honolua Bay can be extremely dangerous, so for your safety please heed all posted signs and use caution.
